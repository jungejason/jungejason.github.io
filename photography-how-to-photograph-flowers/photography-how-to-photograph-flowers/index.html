Tips and Technologies about How to Photograph Flowers

I enjoy taking photos of flowers because I like the colors, shapes, and details present in the photos. I am especially fond of the process of flower photography, and the feeling of satisfaction I get in return.

I have some useful tips and tricks about how to photograph flowers. Enjoy!

Tips 1: Framing

If the background provides some kind of frame, it generally looks good.

Example 1. The tree is blind a fence, but some of the flowers come out. The fence is a good frame that makes the picture very nice.

Tip 2: Stop down for close-up

When taking close-up photos, don’t use the widest aperture. It’s too shallow. It’s hard to get the whole flower in focus. It’s hard to get the whole flower sharp.

Counter Example. I like the composition, but the flower is not sharp enough. With the large aperture, it’s impossible to get the whole flower sharp. (Focus stacking is one solution which will be discussed later.)

Tip 3: Use focus stacking to make the whole flower sharp

Take several photos with different focus then use focus stacking to make the whole flower sharp. It generally time-consuming because you probably need tripod. But sometime hand-held is also OK.

Example: this is from stacking two photos. These were taken hand-held and stacked in Affinity Photo 2.

Tip 4: Balance the composition

Example: the three buds are balanced in this photo. It was actually cropped a little to achieve this balance.

Tip 5: Use contrast background to make the flower pop

For most cases, use a dark background would be good. But overall, the flower and the background should have a good contrast.

Counter example. In this picture, the background is bright while the subject is dark. I tried several way sto edit it but couldn’t make it look good.

Example: The photo in tip 4 looks good because the background color, green, is contrasty to the flower color, and the bacnkground is also darker.

Tip 6: Use blue sky, green leaves, red bricks as background

Example: The red leaves in the background make the flower pop.

Tip 7: Use high-resolution to make room for cropping

It is very useful to use high resolution. Cropping afterward is very useful to improve the composition.

Example. Before the cropping, this is a photo of 9504 x 6336 (61mp). The composition is not very good. Note the image shown below is compressed.

After the cropping to 2302 x 3092.

Tip 8: Use a prominent subject

Do not cover too much. Focus on one subject, or several that form a group.

In the original photo in tip 7, before cropping above, there are no subject. But after the cropping, the only flower becomes the obvious subject.

Tip 10: find flowers in good shape

In most cases, it’s much nice to lok at a flower that’s decaying.

Tip 11: Use Prime Lenses

Prime lenses are generally sharper than zoom lens. They also have larger aperture which is good for close-ups.

The following are taken with Sony ZA 50mm f1.4 lens, cropped.

Tip 12: Use Vintage Lenses for characters

Vintage lenses do have characters (optical defects). It’s fun to use them to take photos of flowers.

This is from Super Takumar 135mm f/2.5. It not super sharp, but I like the color and the bokeh.

Tip 13: Use Macro Lenses or extension tubes for Close-ups

Flowers is a good subject for close-ups. Macro lenses or extension tubes are very useful.

The following is the Super Takumar 135mm f/2.5 with a m42 to e-mount helocoid adapter that can change the focus distance.

The lens’ minimum focus distance is 1.5m which is too far.

Tip 14: Don't Mix Similar Color Background With the Subject

When the background has similar color objects mixing with the subject, it’s hard to make the subject pop. This is a easy mistake to make because other flowers in the background are likely having same color.

The following is an example where some object in the background mixes with the subject.

The part in the red circles are mixing with the subject. It’s almost impossible to make the subject pop in post-processing. I really like this flower, but I can’t do much about it.

Tip 15: Use RAW Format to Show More Details

It’s very common that some color’s surface is smooth the the color doesn’t show much contast and details. It will be much easier to restore some more details if the picture was taken in the RAW format.

The second photo is taken in RAW format and has more details after post-processing.
