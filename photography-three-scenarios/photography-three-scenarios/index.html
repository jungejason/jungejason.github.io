Three Scenarios for Photography

Today I had a chat with an eBay seller about why he switched from Nikon to Leica. The reason he told is that he felt that the modern day camera becomes to be like a computer instead of a comera. It’s getting too complicated. He wants to enjoy the pure photographing experience which. The aligned with the story of an old friend of me. The friend told me that he likes the “mechanic” experience of the Leica system. I didn’t understand it well at that time because I was new to photography. But now, after playing with cameras and lenses for one and a half years, I start to understand it. It’s to take control of the basic of photographing: aperture, ISO, shutter speed, and focus. Then composition.

I personally like manually control as well. But I do need auto system for kids running around. And I sometimes need a quick mode to record life. So I think there are three scenarios for photography for me:

  1. Auto Scenario: kids running around. Use the auto mode and whatever way the camera can provide me
  2. Manual Scenario: take a photo nicely. Use total manual mode
  3. Quick Scenario: recording life quickly.

Auto Scenario

For the Auto Scenario, I practiced it today with intention. I took the Sony Z7Rv with just one lens, Zeiss ZA 35mm f/1.4 when the family went out for lunch in an India restaurant. I wanted to take some good photos for the kids. And it worked out.

Manual Scenario

For the Manual Scenario, I played with all the three Sony bodies I have, and can set up a manual mode which I control everything with as fewer steps as possible. It’s idea to use dials to control the aperture, shutter speed, and ISO. It worked out OK on NEX-6 and ZV-E10, and worked out very well on A7Rv. The following are two shots I took with NEX-6 and Sigma 18-50mm f/2.8.

Photo 1:

It’s in the morning and a little cloudy. The light is not great but OK. The flower is in great shape and has great background. So it’s worth a shot. First I set the shutter speed to 100 to make sure it’s stable enough. Then I set the ISO to as close to 100 as possible, which ends with 400. I want to get a blur background and a sharp enough flower, so I tried to stop down from the largest aperture. It turned out that f/5.6 is good. The result is OK.

Photo 2:

It’s the same morning. The backyard looks clean and this plant looks good. I want to put the tree as a blur foreground. The tree has some gree leaves on the trunk which looks not bad. I chose f/5.6 again, but I think I should have chosen a larger aperture as I’m pretty far from the subject.

I also tried the DMF focus mode in NEX-6. I think it’s renamed to DAF in later Sony model. It’s faster than pure manual mode if the lens supports autofocus. Holding down the shutter button halfway will make it autofocus, then manually adjust a little bit then press the button the whole way.

Things Learned: It’s good to slow down and take the shots slowly. I enjoyed the process more and learned more. I didn’t take as many pictures but Less is More.

The performance of NEX-6 is more than enough for most cases. Unless I want to take super sharp photos with great scenaries and/or great memories, or when I want to crop a lot, it’s totally good enough. Sotoring the files are easier. Processing them on my laptop is much faster and pleasant.

Quick Scenario

For the Quick Scenario, I need a real pocket camera. Ricoh GR iii seems to be the best option. The popular Fujifilm X100 seems to be good, but after careful check, it’s actually not a real pocket camera. If I have to use a strap to carry it, then it overlaps with the existing camera body I have. The NEX-6 is small and with some good lens it’s not that much bigger than the Fujifilm X100 camera for my use case. I can even use A7Rv for serious street photography. Because I don’t do the quick mode that often, I can take my time to find a good deal for Ricoh GR iii.

I used the Aperture Priority mode for all these. It’s effective.

Conclusion

If I stick to the three scenario, I’m skipping one scenario which I have often been doing: try to use auto functionalities to take good photos and play with lenses. I should substitute it with the Manual Scenario instead in which I can enjoy the pure joy of photography more. Less is more.
